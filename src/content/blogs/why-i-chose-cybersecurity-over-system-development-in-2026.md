When I entered my junior year in college, I had to make one of the biggest academic decisions of my degree: choosing my specialization.

## The path I expected to take

For the longest time, the answer seemed obvious. I was going to take **System Development**.

I have always enjoyed building software. From writing code to designing web applications, software engineering was the field I imagined myself pursuing. During my first two years in college, most of my projects revolved around creating applications and solving problems through development.

However, something changed during my sophomore year.

## What changed

I became increasingly fascinated by **machine learning, data science, and AI**. I realized that I enjoyed understanding data, designing intelligent systems, and solving analytical problems more than writing application code itself. Building software became a means to an end rather than the part I was most passionate about.

At the same time, the software engineering landscape was evolving rapidly. AI-assisted development and agentic coding tools were making implementation significantly faster. Writing code was no longer the only bottleneck; understanding problems, designing solutions, and making sound engineering decisions were becoming even more valuable.

That realization made me question whether specializing in System Development was the right path for me.

This was not because I believed software development had become less important. On the contrary, great software engineers are more valuable than ever. Instead, I felt that my personal growth in traditional application development had started to plateau. I wanted a field that would continuously challenge me while still allowing me to apply my interests in AI and data.

## Why cybersecurity stood out

That is where **Cybersecurity** stood out.

Cybersecurity is fundamentally about protecting systems, understanding how they fail, and thinking like both a defender and an attacker. While AI has become an incredibly powerful tool, securing systems still requires human judgment, context, and strategic decision-making. The field constantly evolves because attackers evolve as well.

The curriculum was another important reason. The Cybersecurity specialization at my university still provided opportunities to study **machine learning and data science**, while allowing me to dive deeper into networking, system security, and digital defense. It felt like the right balance between my growing interest in AI and my desire to build expertise in a field where analytical thinking is essential.

## Not leaving software development behind

Looking back, choosing Cybersecurity was not about walking away from software development. It was about recognizing where my curiosity naturally led me.

I still enjoy building systems, and I believe every cybersecurity professional benefits from strong software engineering fundamentals. Today, however, I am more excited about understanding how systems can be secured, how intelligent models can strengthen defense, and how AI can be responsibly integrated into cybersecurity.

Choosing a specialization is rarely about abandoning one passion for another. Sometimes, it is simply about following the direction where your curiosity keeps pulling you.

For me, that direction was cybersecurity.
